Atomic Models Explained

A Journey Through the Evolution of Atomic Theory

Dalton's Atomic Model (1803)

John Dalton proposed that matter is made of indivisible atoms, which are identical for each element but different between elements. Atoms combine in fixed ratios to form compounds.

  • Atoms are solid, indestructible spheres.
  • Each element has atoms of a specific mass.
  • Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms.

Limitations: Did not account for subatomic particles or isotopes.

Thomson's Plum Pudding Model (1897)

J.J. Thomson discovered electrons and proposed that atoms are positively charged spheres with negatively charged electrons embedded within, like plums in a pudding.

  • Atoms contain both positive and negative charges.
  • Electrons are scattered throughout a positive matrix.
  • Atom is neutral overall.

Limitations: Failed to explain atomic stability or electron arrangement.

Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

Ernest Rutherford's gold foil experiment revealed that atoms have a dense, positively charged nucleus with electrons orbiting around it, like planets around the sun.

  • Nucleus contains most of the atom's mass.
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus at a distance.
  • Atom is mostly empty space.

Limitations: Could not explain why electrons don't spiral into the nucleus.

Bohr's Model (1913)

Niels Bohr refined Rutherford's model by proposing that electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels, or shells, and can jump between them by absorbing or emitting energy.

  • Electrons move in quantized orbits.
  • Energy levels correspond to specific electron orbits.
  • Explains atomic spectra.

Limitations: Only accurate for hydrogen-like atoms.

Developed by Schr√∂dinger and others, this model uses quantum mechanics to describe electrons as probability clouds (orbitals) rather than fixed orbits, based on wave-particle duality.

  • Electrons exist in orbitals, regions of probability.
  • Described by quantum numbers (n, l, m, s).
  • Accounts for complex atoms and chemical bonding.
